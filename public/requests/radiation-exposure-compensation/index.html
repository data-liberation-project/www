The Radiation Exposure Compensation Act, passed by Congress in 1990 and amended several times since, “provides one-time benefit payments to persons who may have developed cancer or other specified diseases after being exposed to radiation from atomic weapons testing or uranium mining, milling, or transporting,” according to a helpful explainer from the Congressional Research Service.

The Department of Justice’s Civil Division administers the claims process. As of September 2022, the agency has received more than 53,000 claims. It has approved more than more than 39,000 of them, awarding more than $2.5 billion in compensation. More than 13,000 claims have been denied. But very little information is publicly available on claimantsâ€™ specific illnesses and/or injuries, counties of exposure, employers, reasons for claim denial, or many other aspects of the information collected. Such information is essential to understanding the enduring effects of radiation on the US population and the functioning of this government program.
